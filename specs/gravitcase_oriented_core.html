Joint Core — Oriented to the Datum

Looking down the +X (shaft) axis at the Y–Z plane. Every part shown angled the way it actually points.

The change I found by looking closely: the pawl moves from the bracket to the hub. The trigger rod lives in the spine — it rides with the moving handle. A pawl bolted to the fixed bracket would force that push to cross the moving/fixed boundary, which a push-only rod can't do cleanly. Mounting the pawl on the hub fixes it two ways: the trigger (also on the hub) lifts the pawl directly, and you hold the squeeze through the whole stow swing so it stays clear — and the fold-back load is already in the hub (it comes from the handle), so handing it to a fixed bracket abutment is the natural path, not an extra transfer. So: hub carries the moment; the hub stop lug meets the fixed wall for the open load; the hub pawl catches the fixed abutment for the fold-back. Both stops are hub-feature-into-fixed-bracket-feature. This supersedes the "pawl on the bracket" arrangement in the two prior pawl/bracket sheets.
Orientation check, each part: shaft along X (side-to-side) ✓ · handle/boss radial in the Y–Z plane, 0°=+Z, 45° toward +Y ✓ · stop lug points −Z into the floor at deploy ✓ · pawl on hub, fold-back (+Y) side of the lug ✓ · friction disc on the +X hub face, axis = shaft ✓ · drawbar on the same shaft, lower arc, −Z stowed → +Y deployed at COM ✓.

Interference to resolve in the model: at deploy the lug + pawl sit at −Z, and that's also where the drawbar stows (−Z, folded to the lower-back channel). They share the −Z region — clearance comes from radius and the drawbar lying flat against the back panel, but the assembly sweep must confirm it.
