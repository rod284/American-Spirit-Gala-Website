 3 · Sizing checks 
 Check Design (5,500 N) Ultimate (15,000 N) Verdict 
 Pin double shear (Ø6, 2×28.3 mm²) ~97 MPa ~265 MPa Ample vs hardened-steel shear yield (~660 MPa) 
 Nose contact (50 mm²) ~110 MPa ~300 MPa Well under hardened bearing limit 
 Release force (vs ~40 N spring) rod push ~60 N · trigger delivers ~300 N Ample margin 
 All three are comfortable, which is the point of aiming the load into the pin: the part isn't stress-limited at the design load, so the remaining work is fatigue and the abuse case — FEA territory. 
 4 · Self-locking geometry 
 The trailing-face contact normal is aimed at the pivot-pin center → the fold-back load passes straight into the pin as shear , with negligible moment to rotate the pawl. 
 A backstop face on the bracket catches any residual seating moment, so the pawl cannot be cammed open by load — no matter how hard the hit. 
 The only way out is the wedge lifting the heel, which acts from the opposite side and fights only the spring. 
 5 · Choreography 
 Deploy: squeeze (stow hook releases, friction backs off, pawl held up by the wedge) → swing to 45° → release squeeze → pawl drops behind the lug, lug seats on the fixed wall. Fail-safe backup: the 35° ramp lets the lug ratchet the pawl even if you don't hold the trigger. 
 Tow: hard stop blocks opening; pawl blocks fold-back. A rock-hit loads the pin in shear — held. 
 Stow: squeeze → wedge lifts the pawl out → swing down → stow hook re-engages at 0°. 
 6 · Materials — production vs prototype 
 Production: lug + fixed wall + pawl in hardened alloy steel; Ø6 hardened dowel; spring steel. VALIDATE grades. 
 Prototype (3D print): print the pawl, lug, pocket, wedge in tough plastic (PETG / nylon / resin) to prove fit and motion only. Orient so layer lines run across the nose load, not along it — a printed nose with layer lines parallel to the shear plane will delaminate. The print will not survive the 5,500 N hold; that's expected and not its job. 
 7 · Zoo (KittyCAD KCL) plan 
 Parametric off the hub: drive lug radius (20), widths (10), nose face (5), pivot Ø (6), ramp angles (35/33) from named parameters so a tweak propagates. 
 Zookeeper one step at a time, confirm visually ; lowercase pi ; absolute angular positions for the lug at 45° (don't mirror). 
 Order: (1) pivot pin. (2) pawl body — nose, ramp, bore, heel. (3) hub lug as a feature on the existing hub. (4) fixed wall + backstop on the bracket. (5) wedge on the rod. (6) assemble at 45°, then sweep the hub 0→45° and check the lug clears the ramp and seats — that motion check is the whole point.

 Where this leaves you: the pawl is fully dimensioned and self-locking by geometry, the loads land in a Ø6 pin with comfortable margin, and the release is a light push the trigger already commands. Model it in Zoo off these numbers, print it to confirm the motion, then cut the load-bearing faces in hardened steel for the bench. The three open numbers — fold-back impact magnitude, steel grade/hardness, and spring rate — are the only things between this and a tooled part.
