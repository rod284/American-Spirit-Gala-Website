Load Specification

Gravitcase · 22″ case (560×356×229mm) · pivot core · Rev A · the design basis everything sizes from

The gate. Adopted from real luggage test standards, set by three product decisions, and worked into the design numbers the contract engineer and FEA need. Loads and standards are locked; the derived moments are grounded estimates for FEA + bench to confirm.

LOCKED = a decided input — a rated weight, an adopted standard, a test parameter. VALIDATE = a derived number (moment, force) resting on a stated assumption that FEA, a measurement, or a bench test must confirm.

1 · Scope & decisions

  • Applies to: the 22″ case only (this core is not yet common across the size line). LOCKED
  • Rated contained weight: 25 kg — the industry body-test load. LOCKED
  • Design contained weight: 32 kg — modest overload to the airline single-bag ceiling. LOCKED
  • Rated top-deck / handle load: 7 kg — the load the durability standard already assumes hangs on the handle. LOCKED
  • Combined design lifted mass: 39 kg (≈383 N) — case at design max + top load, what the handle carries in a lift. VALIDATE

Rating a carry-on this high over-builds it for its class — deliberate, so the core carries forward to a checked-size common part later without a redesign.

3 · Load cases & derived core loads

Assumptions for the derivations: pivot-to-CG horizontal (depth) offset ≈ 0.115 m; snatch dynamic factor ≈ 2.5×; 45° stop lug radius ≈ 0.020 m; extended-spine grip height above pivot at 45° ≈ 0.74 m. All three assumptions are VALIDATE — measure the offset on a real loaded case, get the dynamic factor off the instrumented snatch rig.

Which case governs — the honest split. Two different cases set two different targets. Case B (snatch, ~110 N·m) sizes the part for rated life: no yield, full 10,000-cycle fatigue, structural factor of safety ~1.5–2 on yield. Case F (hard yank, ~300 N·m) sizes the ultimate: the stop and hub must survive a furious tug on a jammed case without a sudden, injurious break — permanent deformation may be acceptable there, sudden fracture is not. FEA confirms which one drives each feature; the yank case is ~2.7× the snatch and almost certainly governs ultimate strength of the 45° stop.

4 · Core design targets (what these numbers size)

  • 45° hard stop + hub stop arm: design to ~110 N·m (snatch) with FoS, survive ~300 N·m (yank) without fracture. Hardened faces, even seating. VALIDATE
  • Hub, boss, S1 root, cross-pins: transmit the same moment; the ≤0.05 mm boss fit keeps it in bearing, not slop. VALIDATE
  • Friction pack: holds the angle only — a few N·m to stop drift and flop; the stop carries deploy load. Light enough to squeeze-release with a weak hand. VALIDATE
  • Stow hook + structure: survive the 90 cm drop with the handle stowed. VALIDATE
  • Drawbar + deploy lock: ~200 N pull + a comparable cornering side-load; the concern is rigidity / no long-term slop, not raw strength. VALIDATE
  • Bearings / bushings & spine: 10,000 lift + 32 km roll fatigue without play. VALIDATE

6 · Margins adopted

  • Rated → design: ×1.28 (25 → 32 kg) — your modest overload, "nothing too heavy."
  • Snatch dynamic: ×2.5 assumed (confirm on the rig).
  • Structural: FoS ~1.5–2 on yield at the snatch load; survive the yank without fracture.
  • Top load: +7 kg, additive to the lifted mass.

7 · Open items for the engineer

  4. Material grades/tempers + hardness for shaft, hub, stop, hook; process per part.

Bottom line: rated 25 kg, designed to 32 kg + 7 kg on top, validated against real SATRA/industry tests. The snatch case (~110 N·m) sizes the part for life; the hard-yank case (~300 N·m) sizes it against breaking. Every red number on the core pack now has a parent here — the engineer's job is to run FEA against these cases and tune the three assumptions on the bench.
