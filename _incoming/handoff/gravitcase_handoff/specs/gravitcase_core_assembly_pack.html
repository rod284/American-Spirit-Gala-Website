Core Assembly — Engineering Pack

Gravitcase · COM-height pivot core · Rev A · for contract engineer + CAD + manufacturer hand-off

The single most loaded, most crowded region of the product. Five jobs, one steel core. Geometry and fits are specifiable now; load-driven sizing is flagged for the engineer's FEA and bench test against a load spec we do not yet have.

Reading the tags. LOCKED = geometry, layout, attachment, fit — decided, ready for CAD. VALIDATE = a number driven by force/load — a reasoned starting value that the design load spec + FEA + a bench test must confirm. I will not dress a VALIDATE number up as final; that is how parts fail in the field.

1 · What lives in the core

The COM-height zone (280 mm up, centered on the 14″ width) now carries five functions on one fixed shaft and one rotating hub:

  1. Pivot — the spine swings 0°→45° on the hub.
  2. Angle hold — a light friction pack keeps the handle wherever you leave it.
  3. Load path — a 45° hard stop carries the tow and lift loads (friction holds the angle, the stop takes the load).
  4. Stow latch — a 0° hook on the hub grabs a fixed pin, bin-safe.
  5. Train drawbar — a swing arm on the same shaft, lower arc, tows the next case at COM height.

And the same steel structure extends upward as the brace that carries the slide-stabilizer guides. So this part is not a bracket — it is the structural spine of the whole product. It deserves the most engineering attention and the first, most careful CAD model.

4 · How the latch shares the hub (angular)

The functions don't fight because they occupy different angles on the one hub, and different arcs of its travel:

  • Upper arc (0–45°): the spine boss sweeps here. Clear of everything below.
  • At 45° (outer rim, r≈20): the stop lug meets the bracket stop. The single hardest-loaded contact in the product.
  • At 0° (one side of the hub): the hook catches the fixed pin.
  • Around the hub: the friction annulus, always engaged, light.
  • Below the hub (a fixed feature): the concentrator sits where the trigger rod ends, bearing on both the clamp and the hook so one squeeze does both.
  • Lower arc (separate rotating element): the drawbar swings from straight-down (stowed) to horizontal (locked). It shares the shaft, never the hub's angular range, and never operates at the same time as the handle.

6 · What I must know to make it perfect the first time

You asked me straight, so here it is straight. Geometry I can lock today. But "perfect first time" on a load-bearing mechanism needs inputs I don't have yet. In rough order of leverage:

  1. The design load spec — the #1 gap. Rated case weight, and the test loads & cycle counts: pull-fatigue cycles, lift-by-handle force, drop, lateral/abuse. Every structural number on this page (4500N stop, 300N clamp, ~50mm width, S1 factor of safety) sizes off this. Until we adopt a luggage test standard, those are estimates.
  2. Material grades/tempers + production process per part. Which steel and hardness for shaft/hub/stop/hook; aluminum temper confirmed (6061-T6); and machined vs MIM vs die-cast vs stamped vs molded per part — because process drives achievable tolerance, radius, draft, and min wall.
  3. Friction tuning data. Real μ of the chosen material, clamp-vs-hold curve, the squeeze-release force target, and drift/creep over cycles. Bench, not theory.
  4. User-force ergonomics. Squeeze and thumb force a weak user can apply — sets the trigger ratio for everyone, not the average hand.
  5. Cycle-life targets for each motion (pivot, stow, drawbar, slide) — sizes fatigue and wear.
  6. Tolerance/fit/GD&T scheme + datums — formalized.
  7. Assembly sequence & tool access — prove the axial stack actually goes together and the bolt can be torqued.
  8. Environmental — cold-hold temperature range, corrosion → finishes/plating on steel parts.
  9. Spring rates — friction clamp, hook seat, trigger return, drawbar lock pin, slide detent.
  10. Shell & wheel-base interface — how the core bolts/bonds to the OEM base and how load reaches the wheels (rib runs).
  11. Finishes, fasteners, compliance — anodize spec, thread-lock/torque, any airline or child-restraint standards if claimed.

Items 1–3 are the gates. The contract engineer's first job isn't to draw — it's to set the load spec, because that turns every red VALIDATE number above into a real green one.

7 · CAD / Zoo strategy

Structure it parametric

  • Parent datum = the steel core. Everything references it. Build a parameter table — COM height 280, shaft Ø14, internal 50, each octagon section — so a change propagates instead of breaking ten parts.
  • Sub-assemblies: pivot/hub, friction, drawbar, spine cascade, slide. Assemble into one master and run the interference check on the crowded axial stack — that check is the whole point.

Zoo.dev (KCL) per your learned workflow

  • Drive Zookeeper one step at a time with visual confirmation; raw KCL fails silently (lowercase pi not PI, sketch-on-face instability). Use absolute angular positions for any tooth/feature copies — don't mirror.
  • Modeling order: (1) shaft — simplest, gets you going. (2) the core/bracket — hardest, it's the datum, go slow. (3) hub + boss. (4) octagon sections — parametric repeat, Zoo's strength. (5) drawbar yoke + arm + ball. (6) friction washers. (7) slide + T-top. Then assemble + interference-check.

8 · Manufacturer hand-off package

What a shop needs to quote and build without guessing:

  1. Dimensioned 2D drawings, GD&T, per part — datums, tolerances, fits, surface finish, hardness, material callout.
  2. 3D models (STEP) — every part + the assembly.
  3. BOM — part, material, process, qty, finish, fasteners, suppliers.
  4. Assembly drawing + sequence — exploded view, order, torques, adhesives, fixtures.
  5. Material & finish specs — grades, tempers, plating/anodize, friction material.
  6. The design-load / test spec (§6.1) — so DFM and any tooling are sized to real duty.
  7. Tolerance stack-up analysis — prove the ~50mm axial stack assembles across tolerances.
  8. DFM review per part with the shop — process, tooling, draft, radii, gates.
  9. Critical-to-function list — the four bolded fits in §5, flagged for inspection.
  10. Prototype → tooling plan: Build 1 functional (machined) → Build 2 anodized demo → soft tooling → production. Maps onto your funding tranches.
One-line summary for the engineer: the geometry is ready to model; the loads are not yet specified. Set the load spec first, then this pack becomes a buildable, inspectable, tooled part set — with the 45° stop, the boss fit, the friction clamp, and the drawbar deploy-lock as the four things to prove on the bench before tooling.
